Your RV Sat Parked for Nine Months Last Year. The Renewal Notice Came Anyway.

The renewal notice shows up whether the rig moved or not, and on something that big it is never a small number. Here's why the fees run the way they do on a motorhome, and the established method owners are using to register once and move qualifying vehicles to permanent registration, with plates usually in about two weeks.

Legal Tags Registration Desk • RV, motorhome & trailer specialists • 7 min read

If you own an RV, you already know the rhythm of it. The envelope arrives, you open it standing over the recycling bin, and there's the number again, bigger than the one on your daily driver by a wide margin, for a vehicle that spent most of the last twelve months sitting exactly where you left it. You pay it, because you have to, and then you set a mental reminder to do the whole thing again next year, and the year after that, for as long as you own the rig.

What stings is the arithmetic underneath it. Registration on a big coach is usually priced off the things that make it a big coach, the weight, the length, the value, sometimes the model year. So the nicer the rig and the heavier it sits, the more you hand over annually, whether you put 12,000 miles on it or 300. Two trips to a state park and a Thanksgiving run to see the grandkids, and you're paying like you live in the thing full time.

Then there's the errand itself. The counter, the number ticket, the line that moves at whatever pace it moves at, the clerk asking for a document you were sure you brought. You take a half day off for it, and you'll do it again next spring. Nobody buys a motorhome imagining the DMV as part of the ownership experience, and yet for most owners it's the one appointment that keeps coming back.

The renewal isn't the real cost. The permanence of it is.

Look at it across the life of the rig instead of one line item at a time. Whatever your renewal runs, multiply it by the number of years you plan to keep the coach, then add the inspection some states stack on top of it, the morning you lose at the counter, and the late fee the one year the notice went to your old address. That's the actual price of registering an RV the ordinary way, and almost nobody adds it up, because it arrives in slices small enough to just absorb.

So people try the things that sound like they should help, and they mostly don't:

  • Declaring a lower value. The fee schedule is the fee schedule, and on a rig this size, the weight and length are doing most of the work anyway. There isn't much room to argue your way down.
  • Storing it and skipping a year. Which works right up until you want to actually use it, and then you're paying to bring it current before you can turn a wheel, sometimes with penalties stacked on.
  • Selling and buying smaller. A real cost dressed up as a solution. You lose the rig you actually wanted, and the smaller one still gets a renewal notice every year.
  • Just eating it. Which is what most owners land on, because the alternative feels like it must involve moving to another state, and it doesn't.

And that last assumption is the one worth taking apart, because it's the only thing standing between a lot of owners and a considerably simpler way to own a motorhome.

The rig depreciates. The renewal doesn't. Every year it asks again, and every year the answer has to be yes.

What changes everything: a vehicle can be owned by a company, and companies can be based anywhere.

This is the part that sounds too simple the first time you hear it. Your RV does not have to be registered by you, personally, in the state where you sleep. It can be legally owned and registered by a holding company, and that company can be based in a state whose registration system was built for exactly this: titling vehicles on proof of ownership, with no sales tax, no annual state inspections, and permanent registration available on qualifying vehicles. Once the coach belongs to the entity, the annual cycle you've been feeding is simply no longer the process you're in.

Collectors, exotic-car owners, and full-time RVers have been registering this way for decades. It is not new, it is not clever, and it is not a gray area. It's a legal structure that happens to be a much better fit for a vehicle that spends nine months a year parked than the annual-renewal model most people default into. The mechanics matter, so here they are.

The mechanism

The holding-company method, in plain English

The RV is registered through a Montana holding company, an LLC that legally owns it on paper. Montana titles and registers vehicles based on that ownership, and it does so with no sales tax and no annual state inspections. For light vehicles 11 or more years old, the registration can be made permanent (qualifying vehicles): one payment, no renewal notice next year, none the year after that. This is the same structure that has been used on motorhomes, exotic cars, and collector vehicles for decades, by owners who simply wanted a cleaner registration.

Read that eligibility line carefully, because we'd rather you hear it from us than find out later. Permanent registration is not available on every rig, it applies to qualifying vehicles only, and the age and classification of your specific coach determine whether yours is one of them. Plenty of RVs on the road right now are well past the 11-year mark and qualify comfortably. Plenty of newer ones don't yet, and for those the structure still does its two biggest jobs anyway: no annual state inspections to schedule, fail, and redo, and no sales tax on the registration, which on an $80,000 coach at a 9% rate is roughly $7,200 if you're registering a recent purchase. Permanent registration (qualifying vehicles) stays on the table as the rig ages into it, and unlike the renewal notice, that's a date that only gets closer. The questionnaire is what tells you which side of that line your rig falls on, before you've spent a dollar.

Done on your own, the moving parts are real. You'd be forming the LLC correctly, filing the registration under it, handling the title paperwork, and keeping the whole thing in order over time.
